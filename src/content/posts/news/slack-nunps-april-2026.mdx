I also include prose feedback questions. In general, the themes of positive and negative comments have remained the same all year.

**Positive Feedback**
- Great community and people
- Useful resources
- Great place to build things
- YSWS and hackathons are amazing

**Negative Feedback**
- Navigating the channels is overwhelming
- I wish there were a channel for X. (In general, this channel already exists, meaning they couldn't find it)
- It's hard to connect with people
    - It's hard to connect with people who speak my (non-English) language
    - It's hard to connect with people from my country/region
- Once I'm here, it's not clear what I'm supposed to *do*

**What We've Done So Far**

I'm always trying to improve the new user experience. Lucky for me, you all step up to help. Here are some of the things we've done:
- [slack.hackclub.com](https://slack.hackclub.com)
- [readme.hackclub.com](https://readme.hackclub.com)
- [wiki.hackclub.com](https://wiki.hackclub.com)
- [help.hackclub.com](https://help.hackclub.com)
- #help
- #welcome-to-hack-club
- Making new users full members instead of multi-channel guests
- Converting all legacy multi-channel guests to full members
- A team of volunteers with power to promote MCGs to full members
- Automatically recommending people channels based on their geoIP
- Fun events by the Special Activities Division for newly-joined people

Even though we have not "fixed the Slack", I do think these efforts have made a difference. For example, people who say they joined through slack.hackclub.com have the second-highest NPS of any join method, *and* their NPS has increased over time. My theory is that new user NPS is currently dominated by factors outside of our control. But this does not mean it always will be. When we finally find huge improvements to new user experience, then those will become the big drivers of NPS. By analogy, the health of the plants in your yard is mostly due to factors beyond your control, like the weather. But if you weed and water, then their health will mainly be about factors within your control.

**My Wishlist**

People often ask me, "How can I help?" That's really special and I never stop feeling grateful to work in a community like this. Sometimes I have tasks on hand. Other times I don't, because in my mind, there is a disconnect between what I want, and what I think is possible.

On reflection, that's silly. Who am I to say what you can and can't pull off? You keep surprising me. So, here are some things that I think would make the Slack an amazing place for new users, that I have not pursued because I don't know how to make them happen:

- When you join, you are put into a small group of people run by an amazing mentor who proactively helps you find your way
- "Welcome ships" where when you join, you are immediately handed a simple project wrapped in a super easy-to-use tool bundle (*zero* git required) that helps you learn the most basic basics and more importantly proves to you that hey, you can actually hack!
- A big computer game built by Hack Clubbers that really rewards modding. Most of my favorite games--like Rimworld, Skyrim, and Fallout--have a really active modding scene that makes the game twice as good. If Hack Clubbers made a great game where mods were easy to make and also genuinely rewarding, people would play the game, fall in love with the game, mod the game, and fall in love with Hack Club
- Decluttering Slack's startup behavior. Right now, you are prompted to "select your team" (a nonsense request) and then taken to your DMs with Slackbot, which are 50% covered with a giant rainbow popup about how *amazing* Slack's integrated AI features are and you should *totally* talk to your Slack administrator (whoever *that* is) to turn them on. Unfortunately, I believe this behavior is all hardcoded
- A webpage with an easy-to-read map of the 10-20 most important Slack channels, that I can send to people who aren't on the Slack
- A group of people who go around the Slack proactively modelling good conduct. They go into random channels that have mean/lazy Discord vibes and spend a week being helpful, respectful, and thoughtful, so the people in the channel see *why* the CoC is written the way it is
- A mass-archiving of long-dead channels
- More community events: huddles, games, collaborative art projects
- A professional-quality ~90-second video explaining to a new user: (1) Why the Hack Club Slack is worth their time, (2) What they should do once they've joined (pick a project and start making it!) and (3) Where to get help

While we're at it, here are a few items from my wishlist for improving Hack Club in general:

- More contributors to Slacker News (including critics!)
- More readers of Slacker News
- More people using [the forum Ani made](https://flarum.ani.hackclub.app/)
- A bridge between the Fraud Squad's db and my draft of a unified conduct Airtable
- Fraud Squad internal tools display next to a project what YSWS it was for and how many hours the user claimed

Some of these are quite ambitious. Can they be done? Maybe. Perhaps you, dear reader, will be the one to prove they can.
