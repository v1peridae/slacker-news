On April 28th, four Hack Clubbers held a public debate on whether prediction markets do more good or harm. @Rowan and @Sahil argued in favor of the idea that they do more good than harm, while @Eternal and @Maxstellar argued against.

*Editor's note: a recording of the debate is available on Slack [here](https://hackclub.slack.com/archives/C0A2BPB7202/p1778100775148449)*

For the purposes of this debate:

- A prediction market is a platform where people can bet on the outcome of future events. By buying and selling shares in the outcomes, participants collectively forecast the likelihood of events such as sports results, political elections, or entertainment awards.
- This debate primarily concerned prediction markets that use real money, such as Polymarket.

Every debater had 5 minutes to make a statement supporting their point, in an alternating pattern. In between each speech, a discussion was held. At the end, a “free” final discussion was held, then each rebuttal had a maximum of 2 minutes.

*The Case Against: Prediction Markets Distort Reality and Exploit Participants*

Eternal opened the debate by making a point that separates prediction markets from news outlets: unlike news, real-money prediction markets can change reality. They noted the WNBA incident – when someone threw an inappropriate object onto the court, Polymarket created a market on whether it would happen again. It did, repeatedly. Once Polymarket stopped creating those markets, the behavior stopped too. This shows how financial incentives had been actively encouraging the harmful behavior. They also extended this to war. For instance, someone reportedly made $400,000 betting on Maduro's capture. If a bad outcome is profitable, someone may be motivated to make it happen.

Max further built on this idea in his speech. He argued that prediction markets present themselves as fair and centralized, when in reality they are structurally rigged against most participants. He cited a study showing that approximately 70% of all Polymarket trading addresses have realized net losses, while fewer than 0.04% of addresses captured over 70% of all realized profits, a collective $3.7 billion.

Sahil challenged these points, noting that you don't need to bet to benefit. The odds are publicly visible to anyone at no cost.
He also pushed back on fairness as a meaningful standard. A market where nobody knew anything would just be a casino. What makes prediction markets different is that participants each contribute their own piece of the puzzle. In an election, a pollster knows something a donor doesn't, who knows something a journalist doesn't. Different people know different things that contribute to it, and the theoretical limit of this information is that when a significant fraction of those perspectives contribute to the market, a proxy for the "truth" is revealed.



*The Case For: Prediction Markets Reveal Useful Outcomes*

Sahil argued that prediction markets work because the money creates accountability. Bad traders lose money and exit, leaving the market populated by genuinely informed participants. The aggregate result is more accurate and more real-time than traditional forecasters, who are incentivized to tell engaging stories rather than accurate ones. He framed insider trading as a feature; insider information itself factors into markets as a signal that others rely on. When insiders "sell" info, that in and of itself becomes a signal that others can see and understand, therefore democratizing access to that knowledge.

Sahil also cited Waymo as evidence of prediction markets' value. Their internal market was shut down not because it failed, but because leadership didn't want the transparency it produced.

Rowan also pointed out that in the last NYC mayoral election, for example, news networks were calling it a toss-up on election night, while Polymarket had been saying for weeks that Zohran Mamdani had a 95% chance of winning. He also argued that farmers could use commodity price markets to make planting and purchasing decisions more confidently than futures contracts allow. On insider trading, he contended that stock markets and futures markets are equally susceptible, making singling out prediction markets a double standard.

Max challenged the core argument that prediction markets produce valuable information, by pointing out that polls, expert panels, and superforecasters already provide accurate data without requiring ordinary people to lose money. Since only 3.14% of traders drive all credible price discovery, the rest are simply funding an elite that functions no differently than a paid expert panel, except at everyone else's financial expense.

Eternal added to this by stating that certain actors do not merely predict information. They can also create it. A politician controlling a lockdown, or a general controlling a strike, can manufacture the very event they bet on.

**Analysis**
Both sides agreed that prediction markets are gambling, and that insider trading is real. The core disagreement was whether the informational benefit justifies the financial harm to retail participants. The audience narrowly voted against prediction markets—which Rowan and Sahil said they predicted.
