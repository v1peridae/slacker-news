title: Be Nicer than You Have to
---
A community where people are only as nice as they need to be is a crappy place. There are many reasons why.

One: people feel the required level of kindness depends on how they are treated. But this is a matter of perspective. [Here's a story about parents getting into a brawl over seating at a kindergarten graduation.](https://www.13abc.com/2026/05/22/parents-brawl-over-seating-toledo-kindergarten-graduation-ceremony/)  No doubt each of them thought that they were fully justified every step of the way. There’s a [famous study](https://wolpertlab.neuroscience.columbia.edu/sites/wolpertlab.neuroscience.columbia.edu/files/content/papers/SheBayFri03.pdf) where two people are told to take turns poking each other, each time exactly as hard as the other person just poked them. Consistently, the two participants poked each other slightly harder each time, and by the end were jabbing each other. People disagree on what crosses the line. Things escalate. 

Two: other people will copy your example. I recently saw two cases on the Slack where people were being mean to a community member “for their own good”. One, a long-time Hack Clubber who is very nice but whose opinions are sometimes poorly-received. The other, a new Hack Clubber who seems to overestimate their own skills. In both cases, people have been contemptuous towards them. In both cases, I’ve heard this defended as making them git gud: we have to disrespect these guys, you see, so that they shape up to win our respect.

It doesn’t work like that. You don’t get to choose the lesson. What they see is that you are being mean to them. What lesson do they take from that? It could be any of the following:

- People are being jerks because I’m overestimating my coding skills. I should get good at coding, and then they’ll respect me!
- People are being jerks because that is how you get respect. I should be a jerk too, and then they’ll respect me!
- People are being jerks because they are jerks. This place sucks and I’m leaving.

You don't choose the lesson.

Three: silly mistakes are part of learning. Learning to code, learning to build, learning to have opinions about the world, learning social skills. What kind of space is Hack Club? If it is a space where you aren’t allowed to make mistakes–silly, embarrassing ones that are annoying for others–then it is a place you can’t join unless you’re already good.

I sometimes see an incumbency bias at Hack Club. We have a cool thing going, why let new people come in and get in the way? But this is like how people who own houses want property values to stay high, so they fight anything that could conceivably lower property values, then get surprised when their children have to keep living with them into their 30s because there is no affordable housing<sup><a href="#note-1" id="ref-1">1</a></sup>. I want this to be a good neighborhood too, but it has to be one that newcomers can afford.

My point is that what’s best for the people *currently in Hack Club* is not necessarily what’s best for Hack Club. You have to leave behind something sustainable, for the next generation of Hack Clubbers. As teenagers you understand better than most adults what it’s like when people loot your future for their own short-term gains. If Hack Club is unkind to newcomers, then it will be a fun party for a while, and eventually become a ghost town, or a jerk magnet. [Both dynamics are attractor states: once we’re in them, it will be very hard to get out.](https://news.hackclub.com/essays/three-futures-slack/)

So be nice! Be nice to people who you don’t think deserve it. Be nice when most people would agree you are justified in being mean. Whether you are HQ staff or a community member, we will respect it more if you say “I need help dealing with this person” than if you blow up at them and then say later that you were provoked. You don’t have to lie down and take disrespect. You can stand up for yourself, but don’t confuse that with attacking someone else.

Hack Club is whatever we make it. We can make it special, and better than other online communities. But only if we are nicer than we have to be.
